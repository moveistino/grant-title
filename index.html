Billionaire Johann Rupert grant title deeds to South African residents

December 3, 2019December 3, 2019 Christian Smith Uncategorized

In South Africa, it is particularly difficult to grant property rights to poor people because the homes they live in are trapped in complicated systems of land registration. South African billionaire Johann Rupert and his wife, Gaynor, have worked hard with the local officials of Stellenbosch and the Free Market Foundation to expose the legalities of ownership of property in disadvantaged backgrounds. Thousands of land registry have been turned over, more to come.

To provide title deeds to the world’s poor for their land and homes will generate wealth and boost economic development since property titles enable the poor to use their real estate to lend money and start businesses.

Dr. Johann Rupert and his wife Gaynor presented 132 full title deeds to residents of Kaya Mandi, Klapmuts, Kylemore, Franschhoek, and Le Roux. This was achieved by the goodwill of Ruperts and the effort of Cllr Gesie van Deventer, Mayor of Stellenbosch, and her team working in partnership with the Free Market Foundation (FMF) Khaya Lam (my home) on land reform at the historic town hall of Stellenbosch last 21 November.

Master of Ceremonies Tabiso Mfeya (Stellenbosch Director of Planning and Economic Development), Leon Louw (Executive Director FMF), and Temba Nolutshungu (FMF Cape Town Director) also took part in the ceremony.

The FMF has championed the cause of transforming the various forms of apartheid titles found in townships to absolute, unmistakable possession by current owners for over 40 years. To date, Ruperts has funded 1,000 titles in Stellenbosch and 1,000 in Graff Reinet where Dr. Rupert’s father Anton was born. To date, 585 title documents have been submitted in Stellenbosch, there will be more to come in March 2020. The Ruperts have promised another 10,000 titles

In collaboration with Khaya Lam and the Municipality, Johann and Gaynor Rupert are bringing a meaningful difference to the lives of ordinary South Africans and, in particular, the poorest people living in Stellenbosch. The common objective is to bring about a genuine economic and social transformation through property ownership. Once occupants enter the building, they enter the hall as tenants. When they leave, they are homeowners in possession of a fully tradable freeholder tenure, taking their first step toward more real economic empowerment.

In his speech before the title deed ceremony, Johann Rupert claimed that his family was incredibly lucky in the wealth they had accumulated and needed to make use of it for the greater good. “It is easy to give away money but more difficult to find organizations that spend the time to use the funds properly and efficiently,” he said and commended the FMF and Khaya Lam for their ability to do this.
